<!-- OVERVIEW -->
<h2 style="font-size: 1.45em; font-weight: 700; color: #111; border-bottom: 2px solid #e0eaf2; padding-bottom: 6px; margin: 36px 0 16px 0;">Overview</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 14px 0; font-size: 0.98em; color: #3a3a3a;">
  The discovery of new stable materials is a central problem in computational chemistry and materials science.
  Traditionally, determining whether a given crystal structure is thermodynamically stable requires running
  Density Functional Theory (DFT) simulations — a quantum mechanical approach that can take hours to days
  per structure on high-performance computing clusters.
</p>
<p style="margin: 0 0 14px 0; font-size: 0.98em; color: #3a3a3a;">
  With billions of hypothetical crystal structures to evaluate, DFT-based screening is simply not scalable.
  Machine learning offers a compelling alternative: train a model on known (structure, energy) pairs and use
  it to predict stability in milliseconds.
</p>
<p style="margin: 0 0 14px 0; font-size: 0.98em; color: #3a3a3a;">
  This challenge is part of a broader effort to build open, reproducible benchmarks for machine learning
  in materials science, encouraging the development of robust and interpretable predictive models.
</p>

<!-- CONTEXT -->
<h2 style="font-size: 1.45em; font-weight: 700; color: #111; border-bottom: 2px solid #e0eaf2; padding-bottom: 6px; margin: 36px 0 16px 0;">Context</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 14px 0; font-size: 0.98em; color: #3a3a3a;">
  The <strong>formation energy</strong> (E<sub>f</sub>, in eV/atom) measures the thermodynamic stability of a
  crystal structure relative to its constituent elements. A material is considered stable when its formation
  energy is <em>negative</em> — the lower, the more stable.
</p>
<p style="margin: 0 0 14px 0; font-size: 0.98em; color: #3a3a3a;">
  At the atomic level, the energy of a crystal depends on the identity of its atoms (atomic number Z),
  their 3D spatial arrangement, and the periodicity of the lattice. Capturing these relationships with
  classical descriptors or learned representations is the core challenge of this competition.
</p>
<p style="margin: 0 0 14px 0; font-size: 0.98em; color: #3a3a3a;">
  Data for this challenge is sourced from the
  <a href="https://next-gen.materialsproject.org/api" target="_blank" style="color: #1e6fa5;">Materials Project</a> [1],
  one of the largest open databases of DFT-computed material properties, containing hundreds of thousands
  of crystal structures with their associated formation energies.
</p>

<div style="background: #f0f6fd; border: 1px solid #c5dff4; border-radius: 8px; padding: 16px 22px; margin: 20px 0; font-size: 0.95em; color: #2c3e50;">
  <strong style="color: #0a3d62;">A simplified metaphor.</strong>
  Imagine atoms as colored balls arranged in 3D space following a repeating pattern (the crystal lattice).
  Each arrangement has a natural "energy cost" — how much energy is released or absorbed when atoms come
  together to form the crystal. Your goal is to learn a function that maps any 3D atomic arrangement
  directly to this energy value, without running the underlying physics simulation.
  Here: crystal = arrangement of atoms, formation energy = stability score, DFT = ground-truth simulator.
</div>
